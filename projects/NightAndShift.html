Project Description

Inspired by Five Nights at Freddy's and Willy's Wonderland, Night & Shift thrusts you into a tense horror experience where a single moment of inattention can be fatal. This game is a student project developed over 2 months at the University of Quebec at Chicoutimi (UQAC) using Unreal Engine.

The Story: Your former boss is desperate. His staff quit without a word, the diner was packed all weekend, and he desperately needs someone to clean up the mess. Being the good former employee you are, you agree to help out for a few hours. After all, the pay is good and the job is simple: mop the floors, do the dishes, and take out the trash. But in the dead of night, the diner isn't as empty as it seems. Something is roaming in the dark. Something is watching you.

  • Do Your Job: Complete various cleaning chores through a system of dynamic QTEs. Keep your focus on the task at hand, but always keep an ear out for footsteps.
  • Hide to Survive: You are completely unarmed. If the creature gets too close, your only option is to find a hiding spot and pray it doesn't see you.
  • Survive the Weekend: Make it through Friday, Saturday, and Sunday night. The tension ramps up as the nights progress.

My Role & Contributions

My primary role was as a Gameplay Programmer. I developed the entire spawning system for world elements, the hiding mechanic, and the QTE system along with its integration into interactive objects. I also implemented audio and haptic feedback (such as sounds for dishes, trash cans, floor stains, player breathing, and the death scream) and collaborated with the AI programmer to connect gameplay elements.

In addition to feature development, I dedicated significant time to polishing and debugging. This included fixing issues across the project—ranging from UI and class refactoring to level adjustments—and creating the tutorial UI screens by building upon the base UI templates.

Challenges & Learnings

One of the major challenges of this project was managing team dynamics with varying levels of experience in game development and Unreal Engine. This required strong organizational skills and adaptability, often stepping in to compensate for gaps, review work, and debug systems outside my initial scope to ensure we met our goals and delivered a complete game.

On a technical level, this project deepened my understanding of Unreal Engine. I discovered the power and convenience of using Unreal Plugins to create modular systems, some of which I plan to reuse in future projects. Overall, it was a valuable lesson in professional resilience—delivering solid technical work and maintaining focus on the project's success, regardless of personal preferences for the game's genre.
